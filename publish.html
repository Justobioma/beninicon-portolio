Posterity's Bridge

Princess Iyase-Odozi's research into the historic methods and materials of Edo batikmaking provides the viewer ﬁrst-hand contact with this unique textile popularly adorned by the Edo people just a few decades ago. Viewed in a collection, these fabrics give an insight into how members of a royal household in the Benin kingdom adorned themselves. Although the dye colours mostly used were earthy browns, she has extended this a notch further by rendering the batik in predominantly reds and magenta. It is well known that the sources for dye in ancient Benin was from tree barks, Kola nuts and oxide-coloured soft stone which the people processed by grinding, pounding and fermenting. Some women also used these dyes in a mixture with clay to polish earthen ﬂoors and walls. It was the source of pigment before the arrival of the Europeans. According to Chief Okundaye (husband to Chief Mrs Nike Okundaye ),dye pigments were sourced from those 3 materials before the Europeans came. I interviewed him since he has a ﬁrst- hand experience (from his mother who was into this cra). As a young boy he watched as his own mother processed them by pounding, grinding and fermenting (his mother was a known practitioner of this craft in Benin). With the advent of new imported dyes the local practitioners quickly abandoned the hard-to-process indigenous dyes for the quick –to-use foreign dyes. Along with the introduction of foreign dyes came a wider range of colours. This action of theirs gradually led to the dearth of this unique fabric. With this new initiative, Edo batik is set to make a comeback. Princess IyaseOdozi has succeeded in restoring back to the people of Benin – and, by extension, Nigeria - a lost heritage, another indigenous textile they can be proud to export. On these fabrics, one ﬁnds a plethora of motifs, some used as raw images, others more stylised, including crosses indicative of early contact with Christian missionaries.

Benin Art: An Invaluable Resource for Artists in a Time of Cultural Iconoclasm
- Mr Tobenna Okwuosa

It is becoming increasingly diﬃcult to discuss the relevance of traditional art and culture in a contemporary world that privileges the ideologies and ways of the Global North over those of the Global South. In global contemporary art practice, we have also moved beyond the period where Otherness and indigenous cultural elements were the deﬁning qualities of authenticity for modern and contemporary African artists. However, in this neoliberal, postmodern period, there are artists who still explore traditional African artistic practices with great passion; giving them renewed relevance in a time that is characterized by crude iconoclasm. Princess Theresa Iyase Odozi is one of such artists, and she uses this exhibition to redirect our attention to Benin art: its extensive icons and relevance.
Benin art, particularly those made in bronze, ivory and wood, are rich in icons, symbols and motifs. Benin art was the ﬁrst traditional art, in what is today known as Nigeria, to receive international acclaim shortly after the 1897 British Punitive Expedition when objects looted by the British soldiers began circulating in the West. Some Western modernist artists such as Emil Nolde and Max Pechstein were inspired by the arts of Benin. Ben Enwonwu, the foremost modern Nigerian artist, explored ideas in Benin art and tradition. According to Sylvester Ogbechie, Edo culture gave Enwonwu “a stock of images and a cultural identity that he embraced wholeheartedly ... His art showed the inﬂuences of themes and images adopted from this culture until his death.” Benin art and culture have also inspired the works of Bruce Onobrakpeya. From 1978–1984,“Onobrakpeya made series of pictures called Benin vignettes which enact Benin historical symbols.” In the recent times, Peju Layiwola has carried out major art projects that investigated the British Punitive Expedition of 1897 and its lingering consequences. Her seminal art exhibition, Benin1897. com: Art and the Restitution Question, was held in 2010. Although some modern and contemporary artists have drawn inspirations from Benin art, it is yet to be explored in the scale and scope that some traditional art forms such as Uli (Igbo) and Ona (Yoruba) have been explored.
BeninIconography.com is an ambitious project by which Princess Theresa Iyase-Odozi seeks to show the rich iconic content of Benin art. She challenges us to research into the meanings and signiﬁcance of Benin cultural objects, and shows Benin art as a rich resource for creatives in a time of cultural atrophy. Princess Theresa Iyase-Odozi is an artist, cultural activist, and an entrepreneur with a growing proﬁle in the Nigerian art world.

Princess Tessy Iyase-Odozi Daring Attempt at Rekindling History
-by Evelyn Osagie

I have always known Princess Theresa Iyase-Odozi to be audacious. Not only is it daring to live in the Ogun State Suburb, where she resides, she constantly empowers scores of men and women through her art and GreenHouse Art Empowerment Centre in Olambe.
Her attempt at rekindling history through her Travelling Historical Solo Royal Art Exhibition, entitled: Uhunmwun Vbe Ehinmwen and the Master of the Circled Cross in Benin Kingdomhas yet again laid credence to her bravery.
While blending diverse art forms/media with fashion, her exhibition seeks to bring to the world stage Benin Iconographies and bring the world to Benin. It would also positively impact on Benin traditional textile industry, as well as the art and tourism sectors in Edo State.
When she ﬁrst voiced the idea of the exhibition and her desire to have me on her project team almost three years ago, it left me with excitement. It was the making of history and it was an honour to heed the call.
From the crafting to itemising and ﬁne-tuning of the idea, as well as pumping time and resources into the project with so much passion and energy, I watched her brace diverse challenges with undaunted determination. Indeed, on the road to the making of history only the brave remain standing when it's all said and done. Your courage is noteworthy, just as your attempt, brave.
I am proud to be associated with this woman of great talent who discovered the art in me. You are my inspiration and I celebrate you, your courage and accomplishments.
It is hoped that without waiting until the West comes to celebrate her ﬁrst, the government, especially of Edo State; the Ministry of Information and Culture; National Council of Arts and Culture (NCAC) and corporate bodies would see the immense potential in and support this great talent and her epoch-making art exhibition.

Art as a Presence of the Creator on Earth
- Mr Richmond Esimijolomisan Lovven

How wonderfully does the art imprinted on these batiks reinforce the fact that art is indeed the representation of the correspondence of virtue between a creature and the Creator. Art is what the human creature does when via its work it bears on earth an eternally moving fact of a life of the Creator. This motion or moving fact of a life of the Creator I ﬁrst experienced on earth at age thirteen the ﬁrst time I read Shakespeare's "The Merchant of Venice". Read below words, which Shakespeare spoke through Portia, asking Shylock (the Merchant of Venice) to render mercy unto Antonio.
“The quality of Mercy is not strained. It droppeth as the gentle rain from heaven upon the place beneath. It is twice blest:
It blesseth him that gives and him that takes. 'tis mightiest in the mightiest; it becomes the throned monarch better than his crown. His Sceptre shows the force of temporal power, the attribute to awe and majesty wherein doth sit the dread and fear of Kings; but Mercy is above this sceptred sway. It is enthroned in the hearts of Kings; it is an attribute to God Himself; and earthly power doth then show likest God's when Mercy seasons Justice".
Shakespeare's art is the very evidence of his correspondence of virtue with the Creator, whose correspondence is impossible without a correspondence medium in the earthly human body. The very essence of the body's correspondence medium is what Princess Iyase has imprinted on these batiks. Therefore, I dare say that these bartiks bear evidence of a time when art was how the ancient Africans worshipped the Creator. No, the ancient Africans did not see the Creator as the Whiteman in the sky people prayed to cowardly or begged for something. What a renaissance to see art being unveiled as the very religion, which once upon a time bound Africans to the Creator. May artists such as Princess Iyase increase in number and intensity and may we always harken to support their work of the grand renaissance.

Ovia Cult in Benin Religion/Belief
-by Onigho, O.J.

A ccording to T. Cullen Young, "No approach to any appreciation of indigenous ideas regarding God can take any path but that through the thoughtarea occupied by the ancestors”
Ovia cult worship evolved out of one of the Benin ancestors of the distant past. Ovia, t hough previ ousl y human but dead, i s believed to have survived death and is living in the spiritual realm from where it watches over the life of the faithful and upright worshippers.
Ovia cult has its myth known to its devotees/worshippers. This, they learn in the 'ogua' (groove). J.S. Mbiti, in his book "African Religions and Philosophy, asserts that " To African people,.....religious universe is not an academic proposition. It is an empirical experience which reaches its height in acts of worship." The 'ogua' is a symbolic representation of 'Erinmwin' (spiritual or Sacred world or heaven) with all its supposed concomitant impeccable features as against the 'Agbon' (the village or community or earth) with all its worldly absurdities and sins. So, when devotees go into seclusion to worship in the 'ogua', they are said to have gone to 'Erinmwin' where they are expected to learn or acquire or drink a good measure of the energies of the 'faultless' deity; which will help to enrich their character and guide their interpersonal relationship with others which promotes corporate existence. These 'ogua' returnees can now be described as 'new creatures' in transformation and sanctiﬁcation in holiness.
Ovia cult is fundamental to the life of the community. Its tenets/principle are very instructive and proﬁtable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness. 2Timothy Chapter 3 verse 16b. Its emphasis on group solidarity has been a great ﬁllip to traditional corporate existence which enhances the community's values, aspiration and the maintenance of the local identity. Much of the sanctions that manifest harmony and orderliness in the community derive from its tenets.
Morality, according to E.B. Idowu, is basically the fruit of religion and ..... it is dependent upon it. Ovia, in the past, was absolutely believed to have full responsibility of life. Thus, the people implicitly obeyed/yielded to its tenets. And the moral tone of the community was quite high. Ovia cult teaches the 'God's own laws' as contained in Exodus Chapter 20 verses 13 to 17.
Ovia cultural values permit the preservation and propagation of the attributes of the scriptural fruit of the spirit:love,joy, peace,long suﬀering, gentleness, goodness, faith, meekness, temperance ...... Galatians Chapter 5 verses 22 and 23. Devotees are educated/instructed in the cult for the beneﬁt of the larger community/society as the individual member strive to adjust to the standard of his community.

Synoptic Analysis of Princess Iyase Odozi's Artworks
-Mr. Kunle Filani

The exhibition titled The Renaissance of the Images and Symbolism of Benin Iconography is a watershed of some sort for Princess Iyase Odozi. It is signiﬁcant not only for the richness of the formal coordinates of the employed elements and principles of design, but mainly for adopting cultural aesthetics as the research framework for artistic creativity.
In a conscious and consistent move, Princess Iyase Odozi interrogates the semiotics of the ancient Benin empire using the regal personages as major references. She reconstructs and reinvents the dynamics of power of expression and the expression of power inherent in the well organized and articulate Benin kingdom, both in the ancient past and in the continuing indigenous present. A robust ventilation of the Benin /Edo royalty and royal personages recalls associated images and imageries of authority, prestige, dignity, respect, and religion. It certainly also brings back to life the echoes of creativity found in the court art of Benin.
The iconographic contents of her artworks depict the thoroughness of her research into the identiﬁcation of natural images and the conventional imageries associated with forms and motifs of royalty and traditional institutions. She therefore locates and identiﬁes the symbolic signiﬁcance of seemingly ordinary forms, images and patterns in relation to the larger cultural context. She also explores conventional image identity and pontiﬁcates on the coded interpretations.
Having fulﬁlled the iconographic principles espoused by Erwin Panofsky, she goes further to generate and regenerate new forms, signs and symbols that bear compliance to the established conventions of the ancient traditions.
Princess Iyase Odozi engages in indigenous methodologies and techniques of production such as the art of batik making and mixed- media painting. She derives her her forms and symbols from royal appurtenances, altars, totems, court and ceremonial images and commemorative plaques among other things. She engages in the training and retraining of art students and aspiring artists through the workshop system and apprenticeship where necessary. For her, this entrenches continuity in the education and training of interested students and artists. It also allows for continuity and the survival of skills acquisition and the Benin design consciousness in arts and culture.
Her aesthetic preference is no doubt royal and cultural. She adds fresh perspectives to the use of material and techniques and also contextualize her subject matter to the contemporary situations. This dynamic introduction of modern worldview does not allow for fossilized traditions. She avoids ossiﬁcation of traditional culture by articulating design sensibilities even when rooted in indigenous context. She no doubt has proved that she is a worthy princess of the Benin kingdom whose research activities not only cover artistic creativity but also delves into the realm of historical reconstruction.

Prof. Dele Jegede

Theresa Iyase-Odozi's latest project is a massive smorgasbord of well-conceived art events. She is oﬀering a quintuple exhibition that attempts to essentialize the artist as scholar-entrepreneur convoker. First, the project summons scholars to essay on critical aspects of Iyase-Odozi's visual expressivity. Then there is the exhibition component, which showcases sizeable mixed media paintings. The third prospective enunciates art installation. Parenthetically, I note that it wasn't too long ago that the term, “art installation” provoked a quixotic pablum in certain art circles in Nigeria. But I digress. In the fourth visual engagement, the artist periscopes the art of batik and dives full-strength into the visualization of a bejeweled, dazzling articulation of designs recuperated, refurbished, re-contextualized, re-imagined, or simply repurposed from the stock of familiar visual motifs and iconography. Finally, an art workshop will summit this massive endeavor, which is projected to occur in multiple sites across Nigeria. Phew!
Without doubt, Iyase-Odozi's latest calibration gestures towards the past even as it foregrounds the future. She is focused on excavating the formidable but latent heritage of her ancestors to advance a contemporary chapter in visuality, one that inspires the artist to optimize her critical and creative capital and think beyond the narrow scope of art as, well, just art. In this outing, Iyase-Odozi expands the limits of her creative mission by supplementing her visual repertoire with appropriation. And where else would such appropriation come from but her revered Benin ancestry, with its storied legacy? It is a legacy that palpitates in world-class museums today but, at home, continues to fuel the imagination of an emerging crop of young scholar-artists. Here, Peju Layiwola's earlier project on Benin springs to mind. Iyase-Odozi's current engagement aims at paying homage to Iyase N'Udo of Udo, the father who planted the seed of creative occupancy in an enthusiastic daughter. Now, you know the rest of the story, as that inimitable American radio personality, Paul Harvey, used to quip.
Ladi Kwali; Clara Ugbodaga-Ngu; Aﬁ Ekong; Colette Omogbai; Agbo Oshinowo; Nike Okundaye: these are some of the iconic personae in the pantheon of creative goddesses in Nigerian art. As Theresa IyaseOdozi attempts to supplement the growing population of active artists, especially our women artists, as she strives to complement the nascent expropriation of natal iconography, she is richly deserving of the support of all - the public, the media, academic institutions but most especially corporate establishments who could use their support for art to boost their corporate citizenship.
